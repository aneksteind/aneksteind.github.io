Book Review: The True Believer by Eric Hoffer

Posted on June 30, 2020
Tags: book review

I’m not sure if this was totally coincidental or subconscious on my part, considering the present set of circumstances in which the world finds itself following George Floyd’s death, but I found myself reading a book on mass movements. It wouldn’t surprise me if it were in fact a subconscious urge; it’s during times of stress like these where I find myself seeking solace in the form of some authoritative voice, usually written – although this urge of mine is, in my opinion, ultimately misguided and my search inevitably in vain. After all, seeking to understand a process is not a substitute for participating in that process and certainly not a substitute for achieving its goals.

Hoffer begins with a disclaimer:

“The book passes no judgements, and expresses no preferences. It merely tries to explain; and the explanations – all of them theories – are in the nature of suggestions and arguments even when they are stated in what seems to be a categorical tone”

I kept this disclaimer in mind throughout my reading. However, I couldn’t help but feel another urge, one to take some of Hoffer’s explanations for granted – and I fear others could do the same. This is not a critique so much as it is a disclaimer of my own. I would not dare suggest that Hoffer made an attempt to absolve himself of some form of personal responsibility by prefacing his arguments with a caveat and handing the burden of proof to the reader. However, I believe he could have done better to convince me that this wasn’t the case by adding some rigour to those arguments. That is my critique. Despite numerous and occasionally convincing attempts to provide historical justification, I found there to be far too many circumstances under which Hoffer opted for suggestion and argument without rigourous justification.

To illustrate my point, let’s take three random excerpts from text – really. I’ll generate three random page numbers and, based on the remainder, identify the line numbers and corresponding ideas to scrutinize. Here is the Python code for the task:

# hoffer.py
# Python 3.8.3

import random
import sys

random.seed(int(sys.argv[1]))

numbers = [random.random() for _ in range(3)]

# my copy of the book ranges from page 3 to page 207
pages = [n * 204 + 3.0 for n in numbers]

print(pages)

Using this, the page numbers I get with python hoffer.py 0 are:

[175.2620577111098, 157.6226981998217, 88.79660248949239]

So, let’s identify the ideas.

My edition of the text ranges from page 3 to page 207, so the first number would correspond to page 175, line 7. Working our way back to the beginning of the sentence, it reads:

“The fact that mass movements as they arise often manifest less individual freedom than the order they supplant, is usually ascribed to the trickery of a power-hungry clique that kidnaps the movement at a critical stage and cheats the masses of the freedom about to dawn.”

Despite room for objection, let’s take for granted that mass movements “often” result in mitigated freedom (an argument made earlier in the book), and focus on what Hoffer ascribes it to, which is usually a tight-knit group of bad actors. This is precisely the sort of generality that would one would use to lighten the burden of proof; Hoffer fails to provide any form of support – even personal anecdote – for the rest of the chapter, instead offering new suggestions that go unsupported such as “the freedom the masses crave is not freedom of self-expression and self-realization, but the freedom from the intolerable burden of an autonomous existence” and “the reason [those without respect for the individual] prevail is […] that their attitude is in full accord with the ruling passion of the masses”. In the place of any form of justification are weak qualifications such as “usually”, “almost always”, and “not so much” – providing little comfort.

Following the same process, our second location becomes page 157 line 13, reading:

“The inculcated devotions to church, party, country, leader and creed also perpetuate a state of incompleteness. For every devotion is a socket which demands the fitting in of a complementary part from without.”

There is some lost context here, so let me summarize. Hoffer argues that individuals are kept incomplete, thereby making it possible to maintain control over those individuals and to have those individuals perpetually dependent on a particular movement, by means of fulfillment deprivation: limiting social outlets or banning artistic outlets, et cetera.

So, what compelling evidence does Hoffer present to convince us of this idea that “[u]nification is more a process of dimunition rather than of addition”? My concern here is not in the veracity of the argument, although it should have been when I was reading the book. What I’m concerned with is the lost opportunity. Given in this section (102) are no examples of when these sorts actions have occured. Perhaps they’re too obvious to enumerate? Surely Hoffer does not expect to leave us satisfied here.

The final location is page 88 line 20:

“To a religious movement the present is a place of exile, a vale of tears leading to the heavenly kingdom; to a social revolution it is a mean way station on the road to Utopia; to a nationalist movement it is an ignoble episode preceding the final triumph.”

This section is about the necessity of hope to drive and sustain a movement, and the role the present moment has in contextualizing one’s purpose in that movement. My own hopes were lifted when I saw on the next page a few examples of hope driving mass movements, but was quickly let down. In one, Hoffer cites the Donner Party. According to Wikipedia, it was an arduous and, in the end, tragic migration of 87 individuals from the Midwest to California [1]. I’ll admit that I was not expecting “mass” to be inclusive of this size and was certainly not expecting this interpretation of the word “movement”. In another example, Hoffer invokes biblical figures and stories such as Moses and the Exodus which, to say the least, have been subject to doubt. Why reach so far back in time to support such a basic claim? The third and final example Hoffer offers us is that of the Buchenwald concentration camp in Germany:

“The thirty thousand hopeless people in the concentration camp of the Buchenwald did not develop any form of united action, nor did they manifest any readiness for self sacrifice.”

As it turns out, the united action lacking readiness for self sacrifice which Hoffer claims to have not existed has a name, called the Buchenwald Resistance. It originated nearly two full years before the eventual liberation of the camp and consisted of carefully coordinated forms of sabotage and ultimately a violent attempt to seize control [2].

Needless to say, the book greatly disappointed me. The foundations supporting arguments like those presented, and many others I encountered in the book, are brittle. The justifications are low in effort. The depth is lacking. The reason I felt confident in choosing arguments in such a brash and arbitrary manner is because their occurences make up nearly the entire book; had Hoffer orated the same points in a debate, we might have accused him of Gish galloping [3]. Of even greater concern is the urge I personally felt to take some of the book’s more eloquent and rigourously-justified arguments at their face value.

I encourage the reader to choose other seeds and perform the same test with this text or with others. However, I think readers like myself should find themselves lucky to challenge Hoffer’s arguments in this way. It won’t always be so simple. Witholding any assumptions concerning Hoffer’s sympathies or agenda, I think it’s fair to say that rigourous justification is not limited to those with benevolent intent. It’s tempting to find an assertion with a level of justification exceeding some personal and contextualized threshold and conclude that it has therefore earned its way into our collection of facts, our quiver for debate. However, though it may be obvious, it’s important to take it with a grain of salt even still. That of course means this post is no exception! I encourage you to read the book and draw your own conclusions.

I wonder in what capacity, then, Gish galloping can serve as an effective form of propaganda, a numbers game where a crucial fraction of assertions made enter the mind as if it were fact, without properly justifying themselves. I wonder how many others have been or will be finding their hands or cursors drifting, pulled even, towards this title for the same reasons mine might have been, given the circumstances. I wonder to what extent intuitive but necessarily incomplete heuristics and mental models, sourced from books like this, are already serving as the basis for our political discourse, where it still exists. I wonder where I and others will have the awareness to stop confirmation bias in its tracks, and how often we’ll succeed in being wary of the urge to seek solace in the explanations our authorities diseminate, explanations our biases may support.
